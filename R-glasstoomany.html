A Glass Too Many

The night I realized my marriage was unraveling started like any other. I was sitting at the kitchen table, a glass of whiskey in hand, staring at the flickering screen of my laptop. Deadlines loomed, and the stress of work felt unbearable. The amber liquid in the glass was my escape, my way of dulling the edge.

It wasn’t always this way. There was a time when Sarah and I were inseparable, our love built on shared dreams and laughter. We had met in college, two souls who found solace and excitement in each other’s company. Our wedding day was a blur of joy, surrounded by family and friends, promising forever.

But somewhere along the way, the pressures of life started to weigh heavily on me. The demands of my job grew, and the responsibilities of our growing family became overwhelming. What began as an occasional drink to unwind turned into a nightly ritual. I didn’t see it then, but I was slipping further into dependency.

Sarah tried to talk to me about it countless times. She was patient at first, expressing concern gently, urging me to cut back. “I miss us,” she’d say, her eyes pleading. “I miss the way we used to be.”

I would promise to change, to cut back, to be better. But the promises were empty, lost in the haze of alcohol. The arguments became more frequent, our words sharp and hurtful. I’d come home late, smelling of liquor, and find her sitting on the couch, waiting, her face etched with worry and disappointment.

One evening, I stumbled through the door after a particularly rough day. The house was dark, except for a single light in the living room. Sarah was sitting there, a packed suitcase by her side. Our son, David, was asleep in his room, blissfully unaware of the storm brewing downstairs.

“I can’t do this anymore,” she said, her voice trembling. “I love you, but I can’t keep watching you destroy yourself. And I can’t let David grow up thinking this is normal.”

Her words hit me like a punch to the gut. I wanted to argue, to beg her to stay, but the alcohol had dulled my senses, my ability to fight for what mattered most. I stood there, silent and ashamed, as she walked past me, the suitcase rolling behind her.

That night, alone in our empty house, the reality of my actions hit me with brutal clarity. I had let alcohol take control, pushing away the people who loved me most. The weight of my choices pressed down on me, suffocating and relentless.

The next morning, I woke up with a pounding headache and an overwhelming sense of loss. I knew I had to change, but the road ahead seemed daunting. I started attending AA meetings, seeking help and support to battle my addiction. It was a long, difficult journey, filled with relapses and moments of despair.

Months passed, and I slowly began to rebuild my life. I stayed committed to my sobriety, focusing on becoming the person I used to be. I reached out to Sarah, apologizing for the pain I had caused and expressing my determination to change.

It wasn’t easy, and trust wasn’t rebuilt overnight. But over time, Sarah saw my commitment and began to open up to the possibility of reconciliation. We attended counseling sessions together, working through the hurt and rebuilding the foundation of our relationship.

Today, we’re still a work in progress, but we’re moving forward, one day at a time. I’ve learned the hard way that alcohol can destroy even the strongest bonds, but I’m grateful for the second chance to make things right.

And that’s how alcohol almost ruined my marriage, but with hard work and dedication, we’re finding our way back to each other.

-Jasmine
